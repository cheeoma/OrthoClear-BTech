HIP CONDITIONS IN ORTHOPAEDIC PHYSIOTHERAPY

DEGENERATIVE CONDITIONS:

Hip Osteoarthritis:
This means the cartilage covering the hip joint has gradually worn away over time. The hip is a ball and socket joint where the top of the thigh bone fits into a cup shaped socket in the pelvis. When the smooth cartilage wears down the bones begin to rub against each other causing pain, stiffness and difficulty moving. Symptoms include groin pain, outer hip pain or buttock pain that gets worse with walking, standing for long or climbing stairs. Morning stiffness that eases after moving around is very common. It is more common in people above 50 years, overweight individuals and those with a history of hip injury. Physiotherapy focuses on strengthening the hip muscles, improving flexibility and advising on joint protection during daily activities.

MUSCLE AND TENDON CONDITIONS:

Greater Trochanteric Pain Syndrome (Trochanteric Bursitis):
The greater trochanter is the bony bump you can feel on the outer side of your hip. There is a bursa there that can become inflamed causing pain on the outer side of the hip and thigh. This is very common in middle aged women and runners. The pain is often worse when lying on the affected side, climbing stairs or crossing the legs. Physiotherapy includes hip strengthening exercises, stretching and advice on avoiding positions that aggravate the pain.

Hip Flexor Strain:
The hip flexor muscles are a group of muscles at the front of the hip that lift the leg forward. A strain means these muscles have been overstretched or partially torn. It causes pain at the front of the hip or groin that gets worse when lifting the knee or walking fast. It is common in footballers, runners and people who do a lot of kicking activities. Physiotherapy includes gentle stretching, progressive strengthening and gradual return to sport.

Gluteal Tendinopathy:
This means the tendons of the gluteal muscles which are the large muscles of the buttock have become damaged and painful where they attach to the outer hip bone. It causes outer hip pain and buttock pain that is worse when sitting with legs crossed, climbing stairs or standing on one leg. It is more common in women especially after menopause. Physiotherapy focuses on specific loading exercises for the gluteal tendons and avoiding positions that compress them.

Piriformis Syndrome:
The piriformis is a small muscle deep in the buttock. When it becomes tight or inflamed it can irritate the sciatic nerve which passes very close to it causing buttock pain and sometimes pain radiating down the leg. This can be confused with sciatica from the spine. Physiotherapy includes stretching the piriformis muscle, hip strengthening exercises and manual therapy to release muscle tightness.

FRACTURES:

Hip Fracture:
A hip fracture means a break in the upper part of the thigh bone near the hip joint. It is most common in elderly patients especially those with osteoporosis where the bones have become weak and fragile. It can happen from a relatively minor fall. It causes severe hip pain and the patient is unable to stand or walk. Surgery is almost always required. Physiotherapy after hip fracture surgery is very important and focuses on safely getting the patient walking again, strengthening the hip muscles and preventing complications from prolonged bed rest.

DEVELOPMENTAL CONDITIONS:

Hip Dysplasia:
This means the hip socket did not develop properly and is too shallow to fully cover the ball of the thigh bone. This causes the hip joint to be unstable and can lead to early arthritis if not treated. It can be present from birth or develop during childhood. In adults it causes hip pain and clicking. Physiotherapy helps strengthen the muscles around the hip to improve stability and reduce pain.

Legg-Calve-Perthes Disease:
This is a childhood condition where the blood supply to the ball of the hip joint is temporarily disrupted causing the bone to weaken and change shape. It mainly affects children between 4 and 10 years old. It causes hip pain and a limp. Physiotherapy helps maintain hip movement and muscle strength while the bone heals.

NERVE CONDITIONS:

Meralgia Paraesthetica:
This means a nerve called the lateral femoral cutaneous nerve which supplies feeling to the outer thigh is being compressed. It causes burning, tingling and numbness on the outer thigh. It is common in people who are overweight, pregnant women and people who wear very tight belts or clothing. Physiotherapy helps by identifying and relieving the source of compression and giving advice on posture and clothing.

SNAPPING HIP SYNDROME:

Snapping Hip Syndrome:
This means the patient feels or hears a snapping or clicking sensation in the hip during movement. It can be caused by a tight iliotibial band snapping over the outer hip bone, the hip flexor tendon snapping over the front of the hip or a loose piece of cartilage inside the joint. It is common in dancers and athletes. Physiotherapy focuses on stretching the tight structures and strengthening the hip muscles to reduce the snapping.
