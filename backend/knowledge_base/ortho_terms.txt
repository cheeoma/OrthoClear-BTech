ORTHOPEDIC PHYSIOTHERAPY TERMS AND DEFINITIONS

L4-L5 Disc Herniation:
This means one of the cushions between the bones in your lower back has slipped out of place. It can press on nearby nerves and cause pain, numbness or weakness in your back, buttocks or legs.

Radiculopathy:
This means a nerve in your spine is being pinched or irritated. It causes pain, tingling or numbness that travels from your back down into your arms or legs depending on where the problem is.

Paravertebral Muscle Spasm:
This means the muscles running along both sides of your spine are tightening up involuntarily. It causes stiffness and pain in your back and is usually your body's way of protecting an injured area.

ACL Tear (Anterior Cruciate Ligament):
This means one of the main stabilizing ligaments inside your knee has been torn. It causes the knee to feel unstable, painful and swollen. It often happens during sports activities.

Frozen Shoulder (Adhesive Capsulitis):
This means the tissue surrounding your shoulder joint has become tight and inflamed. It makes it very difficult and painful to move your shoulder in any direction.

Rotator Cuff Tear:
This means one or more of the four muscles that hold your shoulder together has been torn. It causes shoulder pain and weakness especially when lifting your arm.

Osteoarthritis:
This means the protective cartilage covering your joints has worn down over time. It causes pain, stiffness and swelling in joints like your knees, hips or hands.

Spondylosis:
This means your spine is showing signs of wear and tear due to aging. The bones and discs in your spine gradually break down causing stiffness and sometimes pain.

Tendinitis:
This means a tendon which connects your muscle to your bone has become inflamed and irritated. It causes pain and tenderness around a joint especially during movement.

RICE Protocol:
This stands for Rest, Ice, Compression and Elevation. It is the standard first aid treatment for muscle and joint injuries to reduce pain and swelling.

Physiotherapy:
This is a type of treatment that uses exercises, stretches and physical techniques to help your body heal, move better and reduce pain without surgery.


PAIN TYPES AND DESCRIPTIONS:

Acute Pain:
This means pain that started recently, usually within the last 6 weeks. It is often sharp and intense and is the body's immediate response to injury or tissue damage. Acute pain usually improves as the injury heals with proper treatment.

Chronic Pain:
This means pain that has lasted for more than 3 months. It persists beyond the normal healing time and can significantly affect the patient's quality of life, sleep, mood and ability to do daily activities. Managing chronic pain often requires a combination of physiotherapy, lifestyle changes and sometimes psychological support.

Referred Pain:
This means pain that is felt in a different location from where the actual problem is. For example a problem in the lower back can cause pain felt in the buttock or leg. A shoulder problem can cause pain felt in the upper arm. This happens because nerves from different body parts share the same pathways to the brain.

Radicular Pain:
This is pain that travels along the path of a specific nerve from the spine into the limbs. It is caused by compression or irritation of a nerve root in the spine. In the lower back it causes pain that shoots down the leg and is called sciatica. In the neck it causes pain that shoots down the arm.

Neuropathic Pain:
This is pain caused by damage or irritation to the nerves themselves rather than to muscles or bones. It is often described as burning, electric shock like, stabbing or pins and needles. It can occur after nerve injuries, disc herniations pressing on nerves or conditions like diabetes that damage nerves over time.

Inflammatory Pain:
This is pain caused by inflammation in tissues or joints. It is characterized by warmth, redness, swelling and pain at the affected area. It is typically worse in the morning and after periods of rest and eases with gentle movement. It is seen in conditions like rheumatoid arthritis and acute injuries.

Mechanical Pain:
This is pain that is directly related to movement and loading of the spine or joints. It gets worse with certain movements or positions and eases with rest or change of position. It is the most common type of back and neck pain and is usually caused by muscle strain, joint problems or disc issues.

FRACTURE TYPES:

Fracture:
A fracture simply means a broken bone. Fractures can be caused by a sudden injury, a fall or by repeated stress on the bone over time.

Closed Fracture:
This means the bone is broken but the skin over it is intact. The broken bone has not pierced through the skin.

Open Fracture (Compound Fracture):
This means the broken bone has pierced through the skin and is visible. This is a serious injury with a high risk of infection and requires immediate medical treatment.

Stress Fracture:
This is a tiny crack in a bone caused by repeated stress and overuse rather than a single traumatic event. It is common in athletes especially long distance runners. It causes localized bone pain that gets worse with activity and improves with rest.

Compression Fracture:
This means a bone has been crushed or collapsed under pressure. It most commonly affects the spinal vertebrae especially in people with osteoporosis where the bones are weak. It causes sudden back pain often after a minor fall or even just bending forward.

Avulsion Fracture:
This means a small piece of bone has been pulled away at the point where a tendon or ligament attaches. It happens when the tendon or ligament is pulled forcefully enough to tear off a piece of the bone it is attached to.

POSTURE AND ALIGNMENT TERMS:

Kyphosis:
This means an excessive outward curve of the thoracic spine making the upper back appear rounded or hunched. A mild kyphosis is normal but excessive kyphosis causes a stooped posture and can lead to back pain. It is common in elderly patients especially those with osteoporosis and in people who spend long hours sitting and hunching over screens.

Lordosis:
This means an excessive inward curve of the lumbar spine making the lower back appear to arch excessively forward and the buttocks to stick out. A normal lordosis exists in the lower back and neck but excessive lordosis can cause lower back pain. It is common in pregnant women and people with weak core muscles.

Scoliosis:
This means the spine curves sideways in an S or C shape instead of running straight down the middle of the back. It can be mild and barely noticeable or severe and causing significant postural deformity and back pain. It is commonly detected during childhood and adolescence. Physiotherapy uses specific exercises to improve posture and strengthen the muscles supporting the spine.

Flat Back:
This means the normal inward curve of the lower back has been lost making the lower back appear flat. It causes difficulty standing upright for long periods and can cause lower back pain and sometimes leg pain.

Antalgic Posture:
This means the patient has unconsciously adopted an abnormal posture to avoid pain. For example leaning to one side to reduce pressure on a painful disc or nerve. While this temporarily reduces pain it creates abnormal stresses on other structures and needs to be corrected through physiotherapy.

Forward Head Posture:
This means the head is positioned forward of the normal alignment over the shoulders. For every centimeter the head moves forward the effective weight on the neck increases significantly. It causes neck pain, headaches and upper back stiffness. It is increasingly common due to prolonged use of phones and computers.

SWELLING AND INFLAMMATION TERMS:

Edema:
Edema means abnormal accumulation of fluid in the body tissues causing swelling. In orthopaedics it commonly occurs after injury or surgery. It can cause pain, stiffness and restricted movement. Elevation of the limb, compression bandaging and specific exercises help reduce edema.

Effusion:
Effusion means excess fluid
