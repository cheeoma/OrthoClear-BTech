ANATOMY TERMS COMMONLY USED IN ORTHOPAEDIC PHYSIOTHERAPY CLINICAL NOTES

SPINE ANATOMY:

Vertebra (plural Vertebrae):
A vertebra is one of the individual bones that make up the spine. There are 33 vertebrae in total stacked on top of each other to form the spinal column. They protect the spinal cord and support the weight of the body.

Cervical Spine (C1 to C7):
This is the neck part of the spine made up of 7 bones. When a clinical note mentions C4, C5, C6 or any number with a C before it, it is referring to a specific neck bone. The cervical spine supports the head and allows the neck to move in many directions.

Thoracic Spine (T1 to T12):
This is the mid back part of the spine made up of 12 bones. The ribs attach to these bones. When a note mentions T4, T8 or any number with a T before it, it refers to a specific mid back bone. This part of the spine is more stable and less commonly injured than the neck and lower back.

Lumbar Spine (L1 to L5):
This is the lower back part of the spine made up of 5 bones. It carries the most weight and is the most commonly injured part of the spine. When a note mentions L3, L4, L5 or any number with an L before it, it refers to a specific lower back bone. L4-L5 and L5-S1 are the most commonly affected levels for disc problems.

Sacrum (S1 to S5):
The sacrum is a large triangular bone at the base of the spine formed by 5 fused vertebrae. It connects the spine to the pelvis. S1 refers to the first sacral level and is commonly mentioned in relation to disc herniations and nerve problems.

Intervertebral Disc:
This is the cushioning pad that sits between each pair of spinal bones. It acts as a shock absorber for the spine. Each disc has a tough outer ring called the annulus fibrosus and a soft jelly like center called the nucleus pulposus. When the disc herniates or slips the soft center pushes through the tough outer ring and can press on nearby nerves.

Spinal Cord:
The spinal cord is the bundle of nerves that runs through the canal inside the vertebrae from the brain down to the lower back. It carries signals between the brain and the rest of the body controlling movement and sensation. Injury to the spinal cord can cause paralysis and loss of sensation.

Nerve Root:
Nerve roots are the points where individual nerves branch off from the spinal cord and exit through openings between the vertebrae. When a disc herniates or a bone spur forms it can press on a nerve root causing pain, tingling or numbness in the area supplied by that nerve.

UPPER LIMB ANATOMY:

Clavicle (Collarbone):
The clavicle is the long bone that runs horizontally from the top of the breastbone to the shoulder. It is commonly fractured in falls and contact sports. It is the bone you can feel running across the top of your chest to your shoulder.

Scapula (Shoulder Blade):
The scapula is the large flat triangular bone at the back of the shoulder. It provides attachment points for many muscles and forms part of the shoulder joint. Winging of the scapula means it sticks out abnormally from the back and indicates weakness of the muscles that hold it against the rib cage.

Humerus:
The humerus is the long bone of the upper arm running from the shoulder to the elbow. The rounded head of the humerus fits into the shoulder socket to form the shoulder joint.

Radius and Ulna:
These are the two bones of the forearm. The radius is on the thumb side and the ulna is on the little finger side. A Colles fracture is a very common fracture of the lower end of the radius near the wrist caused by falling on an outstretched hand.

Rotator Cuff Muscles:
The rotator cuff is made up of four muscles that surround the shoulder joint. Their names are supraspinatus, infraspinatus, teres minor and subscapularis. Together they hold the arm bone in the shoulder socket and allow the arm to rotate. Supraspinatus is the most commonly injured of the four.

Biceps:
The biceps is the large muscle at the front of the upper arm. It bends the elbow and rotates the forearm so the palm faces upward. Its tendon attaches to the shoulder at the top and to the forearm bones at the bottom.

Triceps:
The triceps is the large muscle at the back of the upper arm. It straightens the elbow. It is the main muscle used when pushing.

LOWER LIMB ANATOMY:

Pelvis:
The pelvis is the large bony basin at the base of the spine formed by the two hip bones and the sacrum. It supports the weight of the upper body and protects the organs of the lower abdomen. The hip joints are formed where the thigh bones connect to the pelvis.

Femur (Thigh Bone):
The femur is the longest and strongest bone in the body. It runs from the hip joint at the top to the knee joint at the bottom. The rounded head of the femur fits into the hip socket. A femoral neck fracture is a common hip fracture in elderly patients.

Patella (Kneecap):
The patella is a small triangular bone sitting at the front of the knee inside the quadriceps tendon. It protects the knee joint and improves the mechanical efficiency of the quadriceps muscle. It can be fractured, dislocated or cause pain due to poor tracking in its groove.

Tibia and Fibula:
These are the two bones of the lower leg. The tibia is the larger shin bone on the inner side. The fibula is the thinner bone on the outer side. The tibia carries most of the body weight. A tibial stress fracture is common in long distance runners.

Quadriceps:
The quadriceps is a group of four large muscles at the front of the thigh. They straighten the knee and are very important for walking, running, climbing stairs and getting up from a chair. After any knee injury or surgery strengthening the quadriceps is a major focus of physiotherapy.

Hamstrings:
The hamstrings are a group of three muscles at the back of the thigh. They bend the knee and extend the hip. Hamstring strains are very common in sprinting sports like football and athletics. A hamstring strain causes sudden sharp pain at the back of the thigh during fast running.

Gastrocnemius and Soleus (Calf Muscles):
These are the two main muscles of the calf at the back of the lower leg. Together they point the foot downward and are important for walking, running and jumping. The gastrocnemius is the larger more superficial muscle and the soleus lies underneath it. Both muscles join to form the Achilles tendon which attaches to the heel bone.

Achilles Tendon:
The Achilles tendon is the largest and strongest tendon in the body. It connects the calf muscles to the heel bone. Achilles tendinitis means this tendon is inflamed and painful especially in the morning and during running. An Achilles tendon rupture means it has completely torn, causing sudden severe pain and inability to push off the foot when walking.

Gluteal Muscles (Glutes):
The gluteal muscles are the large muscles of the buttock. The gluteus maximus is the largest muscle in the body and is the main muscle for extending the hip during walking, running and climbing stairs. The gluteus medius and minimus on the outer hip stabilize the pelvis during single leg activities like walking and running. Weak gluteal muscles are associated with many lower limb injuries and lower back pain.

Hip Flexors (Iliopsoas):
The hip flexors are a group of muscles at the front of the hip that lift the thigh forward. The main hip flexor is the iliopsoas which is actually two muscles, the iliacus and the psoas major, that join together. Tight hip flexors are very common in people who sit for long hours and can contribute to lower back pain.

JOINT ANATOMY TERMS:

Cartilage:
Cartilage is a smooth, firm but flexible tissue that covers the ends of bones inside joints. It acts as a shock absorber and allows bones to glide smoothly against each other without friction or pain. When cartilage wears away as in osteoarthritis bones begin to rub against each other causing pain and stiffness.

Ligament:
A ligament is a tough band of fibrous tissue that connects one bone to another across a joint. Ligaments stabilize joints and prevent excessive or abnormal movement. When a ligament is overstretched or torn it is called a sprain.

Tendon:
A tendon is a strong cord of fibrous tissue that connects a muscle to a bone. When a muscle contracts it pulls on the tendon which then moves the bone. When a tendon is overstretched or torn it is called a strain. Tendon inflammation is called tendinitis.

Bursa (plural Bursae):
A bursa is a small fluid filled sac found near joints and tendons. It acts as a cushion to reduce friction between moving parts. When a bursa becomes inflamed it is called bursitis. It causes localized pain and swelling near the affected joint.

Synovial Fluid:
This is the slippery fluid found inside joints that lubricates the joint surfaces and nourishes the cartilage. In conditions like rheumatoid arthritis the lining that produces this fluid becomes inflamed causing excess fluid production and joint swelling.

Fascia:
Fascia is a thin layer of connective tissue that wraps around muscles, organs and other structures throughout the body like a bodysuit. When fascia becomes tight or scarred it can restrict movement and cause pain. Myofascial release is a physiotherapy technique used to release tight fascia.
