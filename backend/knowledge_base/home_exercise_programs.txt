HOME EXERCISE PROGRAMS IN ORTHOPAEDIC PHYSIOTHERAPY

WHAT IS A HOME EXERCISE PROGRAM?
A Home Exercise Program or HEP is a set of specific exercises your physiotherapist designs for you to do at home between your clinic visits. It is one of the most important parts of your physiotherapy treatment. The exercises are chosen specifically for your condition and goals. Doing your home exercises consistently and correctly significantly speeds up your recovery. Your physiotherapist will demonstrate each exercise and make sure you understand how to do them safely before you leave the clinic.

GENERAL RULES FOR HOME EXERCISES:
Always warm up gently before doing your exercises. Never push through sharp or severe pain. Mild discomfort is acceptable but stop if you feel sharp stabbing or worsening pain. Do the exercises at the number of repetitions and sets your physiotherapist prescribed. Do not skip rest days. Recovery happens during rest not just during exercise. If an exercise causes new or worsening symptoms stop and contact your physiotherapist.

LOWER BACK HOME EXERCISE PROGRAM:

Pelvic Tilt:
Lie on your back with your knees bent and feet flat on the floor. Gently tighten your tummy muscles and press your lower back flat against the floor. Hold for 5 seconds then relax. Repeat 10 times. This exercise activates the deep core muscles that support and stabilize your lower back.

Knee to Chest Stretch:
Lie on your back with knees bent. Bring one knee up towards your chest and hold it there with both hands for 20 to 30 seconds. You should feel a gentle stretch in your lower back and buttock. Repeat with the other leg. Do this 3 times on each side. This helps relieve lower back muscle tightness.

Cat Cow Exercise:
Get on your hands and knees with your hands directly under your shoulders and knees under your hips. Slowly arch your back upward like a cat stretching then slowly let it sag downward. Move gently and smoothly between these two positions. Repeat 10 times. This mobilizes the lower back joints and stretches the back muscles.

Bird Dog Exercise:
Get on your hands and knees. Slowly extend your right arm straight forward while extending your left leg straight back at the same time. Keep your back flat and your tummy gently tightened. Hold for 5 seconds then return to the starting position. Repeat on the other side. Do 10 repetitions on each side. This is a core stability exercise that strengthens the back and abdominal muscles.

Lumbar Stabilization Exercises in Crook Lying:
Lie on your back with knees bent and feet flat on the floor. This is called the crook lying position. Gently tighten the deep tummy muscles as if trying to pull your belly button toward your spine. Hold this gentle contraction while breathing normally. Do not hold your breath. Hold for 10 seconds and repeat 10 times. This activates the transverse abdominis which is the deepest abdominal muscle and the most important one for lower back stability.

Back Extension Exercise in Sitting:
Sit upright on a chair. Place your hands on your lower back for support. Gently lean backward extending your lower back. Hold for 3 to 5 seconds then return to sitting upright. Repeat 5 times. This exercise helps restore the normal inward curve of the lower back which is often flattened by prolonged sitting.

McKenzie Extension Exercise:
Lie face down on a firm surface with your hands placed under your shoulders like the start of a push up. Keeping your hips on the floor gently push your upper body up with your arms letting your lower back sag. Go only as far as is comfortable. Hold for 2 seconds and lower back down. Repeat 10 times. This exercise is particularly helpful for disc related lower back pain with leg symptoms as it centralizes the pain meaning it brings the leg pain back toward the centre of the back which is a sign of improvement.

KNEE HOME EXERCISE PROGRAM:

Quadriceps Setting Exercise:
Sit on the floor or bed with your leg straight out in front of you. Tighten the muscle at the front of your thigh by pushing the back of your knee down toward the floor. You should see the muscle contract and the kneecap move slightly. Hold for 5 seconds then relax. Repeat 10 times. This exercise activates the quadriceps muscle without putting stress on the knee joint. It is especially important after knee surgery or injury when the muscle has become weak.

Straight Leg Raise:
Lie on your back. Bend the uninvolved knee so that foot is flat on the floor. Keep the involved leg straight and tighten the thigh muscle. Slowly raise the straight leg to about 45 degrees from the floor. Hold for 2 seconds then slowly lower it. Repeat 10 to 15 times. This strengthens the quadriceps and hip flexors without stressing the knee joint.

Short Arc Quads:
Lie on your back or sit with a rolled towel or pillow under the involved knee to support it at about 30 to 40 degrees of bend. From this position straighten the knee fully by contracting the thigh muscle. Hold for 5 seconds then slowly lower. Repeat 10 times. This strengthens the final degrees of quadriceps contraction which is important for activities like climbing stairs and getting up from a chair.

Terminal Knee Extension:
Stand with a resistance band looped around the back of the involved knee. Face away from where the band is attached. Step forward slightly to create tension in the band. From a slightly bent knee position straighten the knee fully against the resistance of the band. Hold for 2 seconds then slowly return. Repeat 15 times. This is a functional strengthening exercise for the quadriceps.

Knee ROM Exercise in Prone Position:
Lie face down on a firm surface. Slowly bend the knee by bringing the heel toward the buttocks as far as comfortable. Hold for 2 seconds then slowly lower. Repeat 10 times on each side. This exercise improves knee flexion range of motion which is often lost after knee surgery or injury.

Isometric Quadriceps Exercise:
Sit with the leg straight. Press the back of the knee down firmly into the surface below. Hold this contraction for 10 seconds then relax. Repeat 10 times. Do 2 to 3 sets. This is another way to strengthen the quadriceps without joint movement and is useful in the early stages after injury when the knee is painful.

SHOULDER HOME EXERCISE PROGRAM:

Pendulum Exercise:
Stand next to a table and lean forward supporting yourself with the unaffected arm. Let the affected arm hang freely. Using your body weight to create movement gently swing the arm in small circles both clockwise and anticlockwise. Then swing it forward and backward and side to side. Do each direction for 30 seconds. This exercise uses gravity to gently distract the shoulder joint and reduce pain.

Wall Ladder Exercise:
Stand facing a wall at arm's length. Use your fingers to walk your hand up the wall as high as you comfortably can. Hold for 5 seconds at the highest point then walk the hand back down. Repeat 10 times. This exercise progressively improves shoulder flexion range of motion.

Shoulder External Rotation Exercise:
Hold a light resistance band or a small weight. Keep your elbow bent at 90 degrees and held close to your side. Rotate your forearm outward away from your body keeping the elbow tucked in. Hold for 2 seconds then slowly return. Repeat 15 times. This strengthens the rotator cuff muscles particularly the infraspinatus and teres minor which are important for shoulder stability.

Rhythmic Stabilization Exercise:
Hold your arm out in front of you. Have someone gently push your arm in different directions while you resist the movement and try to keep your arm still. This improves the ability of the shoulder muscles to react quickly and maintain joint stability. It is particularly useful for athletes returning to throwing or overhead sports.

Theraband Shoulder Movements:
Use a resistance band attached to a fixed point. Perform shoulder movements against the resistance of the band including forward flexion, abduction which is lifting the arm to the side and external rotation. Start with a light band and progress to heavier resistance as strength improves. Do 3 sets of 10 to 15 repetitions.

GENERAL MOBILITY AND STRETCHING EXERCISES:

Hamstring Stretch:
Sit on the edge of a chair. Extend one leg straight out with the heel on the floor. Sit up straight and gently lean forward from the hips keeping the back straight until you feel a stretch at the back of the thigh. Hold for 20 to 30 seconds. Repeat 3 times on each side. This stretches the hamstring muscles which when tight can contribute to lower back pain.

Calf Stretch:
Stand facing a wall with both hands on the wall. Step one foot back. Keep the back knee straight and the back heel firmly on the floor. Lean gently toward the wall until you feel a stretch in the back of the lower leg. Hold for 30 seconds. Repeat 3 times on each side. This stretches the gastrocnemius muscle.

Hip Flexor Stretch:
Kneel on one knee with the other foot forward. Gently push your hips forward until you feel a stretch at the front of the hip of the kneeling leg. Keep your back straight and your tummy gently tightened. Hold for 20 to 30 seconds. Repeat 3 times on each side. This stretches the hip flexor muscles which are commonly tight in people who sit for long hours.

REAL CASE HOME EXERCISE PROGRAMS FROM NIGERIAN TEACHING HOSPITAL:

Post Corrective Osteotomy Knee Rehabilitation HEP:
Following corrective osteotomy surgery on the right knee a patient was prescribed the following home exercise program. Free active and assisted active exercises to all joints of the right lower limb meaning gentle movement exercises for the hip, knee, ankle and toes. Isometric quadriceps strengthening exercises meaning tightening the thigh muscle without bending the knee, done in sets of 10 repetitions. Sitting reeducation from long sitting to high sitting on the bed which was well tolerated meaning the patient was being trained to sit up in different positions gradually. Standing reeducation with therapist support. Standing reeducation with Zimmer frame. Walking reeducation with axillary crutches meaning learning to walk with crutches that go under the armpits.

Knee Osteoarthritis HEP:
For a patient with bilateral knee osteoarthritis the following home exercises were prescribed. Cryotherapy to the knee joint meaning applying cold packs to reduce swelling and pain. Bilaterally lateral traction to bilateral knee joints for 15 minutes using 5 kilograms meaning a gentle pulling force to open up the knee joint space. Sustained traction in high sitting for 10 minutes with 4 kilograms weight. Knee joint in high sitting with weight and assisted active exercise. Free active mobilisation exercise. Knee strengthening with quadriceps exercises done in 2 sets of 10 repetitions. Soft tissue manipulation. Patient education on dietary control. Home exercise programme and knee brace recommended.

Lower Back and Knee Pain Combined HEP:
For a patient with lower back pain radiating to the right lower limb posterolaterally the following was prescribed at home. Infrared radiation to the lumbo-sacral region 10 repetitions. Soft tissue manipulation with analgesic cream to the low back and right knee joint with interferential therapy and ultrasound to the knee. Back extension exercises in sitting position 5 repetitions. Active range of motion exercises to both knee joints in prone position 10 repetitions each. Gentle bilateral hamstring stretching passively 5 repetitions each. Bicycle ergometry with resistance at 3 sets of 6 minutes with 2 minutes rest phase after 3 minutes. Home exercise programme including patient education on back care and the need for microbreaks after sitting for one hour.

Shoulder Rehabilitation HEP:
For a patient with cervical radiculitis and right shoulder osteoarthritis the following home exercises were prescribed. Infrared radiation to right shoulder 15 repetitions. Longitudinal distraction and posterior gliding of right shoulder joint. Soft tissue manipulation with topical cream. Wall ladder exercises 10 repetitions. Rhythmic stabilization exercises for the patient to stabilize shoulder movements manually. Range of motion exercises with Theraband. Home exercise programme including patient education on ergonomics and posture.

Lumbar Spine Rehabilitation HEP:
For a patient with lumbar spondylodiscitis the following was prescribed. Vertical oscillatory pressure at L1 to S1 region. Cryotherapy to the lumbo-sacral region 15 repetitions. Soft tissue manipulation. Lumbar stabilization exercises in crook lying position 10 repetitions, 5 seconds each. Side lying with pillows under the pelvis for 30 minutes. Left lateral stretch on the wall bar. Patient education on ergonomics and back care. Home exercise programme.
