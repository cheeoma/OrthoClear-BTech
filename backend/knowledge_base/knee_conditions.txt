KNEE CONDITIONS IN ORTHOPAEDIC PHYSIOTHERAPY

LIGAMENT INJURIES:

ACL Tear (Anterior Cruciate Ligament Tear):
The ACL is one of the main ligaments that holds the knee together and keeps it stable. It runs diagonally through the middle of the knee. An ACL tear usually happens during sports activities that involve sudden stopping, jumping or changing direction quickly. It causes immediate severe pain, swelling and a feeling that the knee gave way or buckled. The patient usually cannot continue playing or walking normally. Physiotherapy after ACL injury or surgery focuses on reducing swelling, regaining knee movement, strengthening the surrounding muscles and safely returning to sports or normal activities.

PCL Tear (Posterior Cruciate Ligament Tear):
The PCL is another ligament inside the knee that prevents the shin bone from sliding backward. It is stronger than the ACL and less commonly injured. It is often injured by a direct blow to the front of the knee such as hitting the dashboard in a car accident or falling on a bent knee. It causes pain, swelling and instability at the back of the knee. Physiotherapy focuses on strengthening the quadriceps muscles at the front of the thigh to compensate for the injured ligament.

MCL Sprain (Medial Collateral Ligament Sprain):
The MCL is a ligament on the inner side of the knee that prevents the knee from bending inward. It is commonly injured when the knee is hit from the outside or twisted. It causes pain and tenderness on the inner side of the knee and sometimes swelling. Most MCL injuries heal well with physiotherapy without needing surgery. Treatment includes rest, ice, compression, knee bracing and progressive strengthening exercises.

LCL Sprain (Lateral Collateral Ligament Sprain):
The LCL is a ligament on the outer side of the knee. It is less commonly injured than the MCL. Injury causes pain on the outer side of the knee. Treatment is similar to MCL injuries and most cases respond well to physiotherapy.

MENISCUS INJURIES:

Meniscus Tear:
The meniscus is a C-shaped piece of cartilage that acts as a shock absorber inside the knee. There are two menisci in each knee, one on the inner side and one on the outer side. A tear can happen suddenly during sports or gradually due to aging and wear. Symptoms include knee pain, swelling, stiffness and sometimes a locking or catching sensation in the knee. The patient may feel like the knee is stuck and cannot fully straighten. Physiotherapy helps reduce pain and swelling and strengthens the muscles around the knee to support it.

Medial Meniscus Tear:
This specifically means the meniscus on the inner side of the knee is torn. It is the more commonly injured of the two menisci. It often happens together with an ACL injury. Symptoms include pain on the inner side of the knee, swelling and difficulty fully bending or straightening the knee.

Lateral Meniscus Tear:
This means the meniscus on the outer side of the knee is torn. It is less common than a medial meniscus tear. Symptoms include pain on the outer side of the knee and swelling.

KNEECAP CONDITIONS:

Patellofemoral Pain Syndrome (PFPS):
This is a very common condition especially in runners, cyclists and young active people. It causes aching pain around or behind the kneecap that gets worse when going up or down stairs, squatting, running or sitting for long periods. It is caused by the kneecap not tracking properly in its groove on the thigh bone. Physiotherapy focuses on strengthening the hip and thigh muscles and improving kneecap alignment.

Patellar Tendinitis (Jumper's Knee):
This is inflammation of the tendon that connects the kneecap to the shin bone. It causes pain just below the kneecap that gets worse with jumping, running and squatting. It is common in basketball and volleyball players. Physiotherapy includes specific eccentric strengthening exercises and load management.

Patellar Dislocation:
This means the kneecap has slipped completely out of its normal position in the groove at the front of the knee. It causes sudden severe pain and the knee looks deformed. After the kneecap is put back in place physiotherapy focuses on strengthening the surrounding muscles to prevent it happening again.

DEGENERATIVE CONDITIONS:

Knee Osteoarthritis:
This means the cartilage covering the ends of the bones in the knee has worn down over time. Without this smooth cartilage the bones rub against each other causing pain, stiffness and swelling. It is very common in people above 50 years and those who are overweight. Symptoms include morning stiffness that improves after moving around, pain that gets worse with activity and a crunching or grinding sensation in the knee. Physiotherapy focuses on strengthening the muscles around the knee, improving flexibility and advising on joint protection strategies.

ILIOTIBIAL BAND CONDITIONS:

Iliotibial Band Syndrome (ITBS):
The iliotibial band is a thick band of connective tissue running from the hip down the outer thigh to just below the outer knee. When it becomes tight and inflamed it causes pain on the outer side of the knee especially during running or cycling. It is one of the most common running injuries. Physiotherapy includes stretching the ITB and hip muscles, strengthening the hip abductors and temporarily modifying training.

GENERAL KNEE CONDITIONS:

Knee Effusion:
This means there is excess fluid buildup inside the knee joint causing it to look swollen and puffy. It is the knee's response to injury, infection or disease. The knee feels tight and is painful to bend fully. Treatment addresses the underlying cause and physiotherapy helps restore movement and strength after the swelling reduces.

Bursitis:
Bursae are small fluid filled sacs that cushion the knee joint. When they become inflamed due to repeated pressure or injury it is called bursitis. It causes localized pain and swelling around the knee. Physiotherapy includes advice on reducing pressure on the knee and gentle exercises to maintain movement.
