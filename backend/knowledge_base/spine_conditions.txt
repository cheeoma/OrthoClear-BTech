SPINE AND BACK CONDITIONS IN ORTHOPAEDIC PHYSIOTHERAPY

LOWER BACK CONDITIONS:

Low Back Pain (LBP):
Low back pain is pain felt in the lower part of the back, between the bottom of the ribs and the top of the legs. It is one of the most common reasons people visit a physiotherapist in Nigeria. It can be caused by muscle strain, poor posture, heavy lifting, prolonged sitting or underlying spine conditions. It can be sharp, dull, burning or aching. Treatment includes exercises, heat therapy, manual therapy and postural correction.

Acute Low Back Pain (ALBP):
This is low back pain that started suddenly, usually within the last 6 weeks. It is often caused by a sudden movement, lifting something heavy or a fall. It is usually very painful at first but improves with proper treatment and rest. The physiotherapist will focus on reducing pain and getting the patient moving again safely.

Chronic Low Back Pain (CLBP):
This is low back pain that has lasted for more than 3 months. It is harder to treat than acute low back pain and often affects the patient's daily life, sleep and mood. Treatment focuses on strengthening exercises, lifestyle changes and pain management strategies.

Lumbar Muscle Strain:
This means the muscles in the lower back have been overstretched or torn slightly. It causes pain and stiffness in the lower back that gets worse with movement. It is very common in people who do heavy manual work or sudden intense exercise. It usually heals well with physiotherapy, rest and heat treatment.

Prolapsed Intervertebral Disc (PID) also called Slipped Disc or Herniated Disc:
Between each bone in your spine there is a soft cushion called a disc. When this cushion bulges or breaks open it can press on nearby nerves causing pain. In the lower back this often causes pain that shoots down one or both legs. This shooting leg pain is called sciatica. Treatment includes specific exercises, manual therapy and postural advice.

L4-L5 Disc Herniation:
This specifically means the disc between the 4th and 5th bones of the lower back has slipped or bulged. It commonly causes lower back pain, buttock pain and pain or numbness running down the leg to the foot. The physiotherapist will prescribe specific exercises to reduce pressure on the disc and relieve nerve irritation.

L5-S1 Disc Herniation:
This means the disc between the last lower back bone and the first tailbone has herniated. It causes similar symptoms to L4-L5 but the pain and numbness often runs to the outer foot and little toe. It is a very common finding in Nigerian adults especially those with physically demanding jobs.

Sciatica:
Sciatica is not a diagnosis on its own but a symptom. It means the sciatic nerve which runs from the lower back down through the buttock and leg is being irritated or compressed. It causes sharp burning or shooting pain that travels from the lower back down one leg sometimes all the way to the foot. It can also cause numbness and tingling in the leg. Physiotherapy treatment includes nerve mobilization exercises, stretching and postural correction.

Lumbar Spinal Stenosis:
This means the canal inside the lower spine bones where the spinal cord passes through has become narrowed. This narrowing squeezes the nerves causing pain, numbness and weakness in the legs especially when walking or standing for long. Sitting down or bending forward usually gives relief. It is more common in older patients. Physiotherapy helps by improving posture, strengthening core muscles and improving walking ability.

Spondylosis (Lumbar Spondylosis):
This means the bones and discs in the lower back are showing signs of wear and tear due to aging. Bone spurs may form and discs may dry out and shrink. It causes stiffness and aching pain in the lower back especially in the morning. It is very common in patients above 40 years in Nigeria. Physiotherapy focuses on keeping the spine flexible and the surrounding muscles strong.

Spondylolisthesis:
This means one of the bones in the spine has slipped forward over the bone below it. It causes lower back pain and sometimes leg pain. In severe cases it can cause difficulty walking. Physiotherapy focuses on strengthening the core and back muscles to stabilize the spine and reduce pain.

Ankylosing Spondylitis (AS):
This is a type of arthritis that mainly affects the spine. It causes inflammation that over time can cause the spinal bones to fuse together making the spine stiff and less flexible. It causes long lasting back pain and stiffness especially in the morning. Physiotherapy is very important to keep the spine as flexible as possible and maintain good posture.

Paravertebral Muscle Spasm:
This means the muscles running alongside the spine on both sides are contracting and tightening involuntarily. It causes the back to feel very stiff and painful and makes it difficult to bend or turn. It is usually the body's protective response to an underlying spine injury or irritation. Heat therapy, massage and gentle stretching help to relieve it.

NECK CONDITIONS:

Cervical Spondylosis:
This means the bones and discs in the neck are showing wear and tear due to aging. It causes neck pain, stiffness and sometimes headaches. In more advanced cases it can press on nerves causing pain, tingling or numbness in the arms and hands. It is common in older adults and people who spend long hours looking at screens or in poor sitting positions.

Cervical Disc Herniation:
This means one of the cushioning discs between the neck bones has bulged or ruptured. It can press on nerves causing neck pain that shoots down the arm. The patient may also feel numbness or weakness in the arm or hand. Physiotherapy helps through neck exercises, traction and postural correction.

Cervical Radiculopathy:
This means a nerve in the neck is being pinched or compressed. It causes pain, tingling, numbness or weakness that travels from the neck down into the shoulder, arm or hand. The specific area affected depends on which nerve is being pinched. Physiotherapy treatment includes nerve mobilization, neck exercises and postural education.

Whiplash:
This is a neck injury caused by the head being suddenly and forcefully thrown forward and then backward, like in a car accident. It causes neck pain, stiffness, headaches and sometimes dizziness. Most people recover well with physiotherapy which includes gentle neck exercises, manual therapy and gradual return to normal activities.

Torticollis (Wry Neck):
This means the neck muscles are contracting abnormally causing the head to tilt to one side and rotate. It causes neck pain and restricted neck movement. In babies it can be present from birth. Physiotherapy uses stretching, massage and exercises to correct the head position and restore normal movement.

Neck Pain with Referred Pain:
Sometimes neck problems cause pain that is felt not just in the neck but also in the shoulder, upper back, arm or even the head. This is called referred pain. It means the source of the problem is in the neck but the pain travels to other areas through nerves. The physiotherapist will treat the neck to relieve pain in all the affected areas.
