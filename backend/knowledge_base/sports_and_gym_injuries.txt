SPORTS AND GYM RELATED INJURIES IN ORTHOPAEDIC PHYSIOTHERAPY

MUSCLE INJURIES:

Muscle Strain (Muscle Pull or Muscle Tear):
A muscle strain means the muscle fibres have been overstretched and partially or completely torn. It is graded from Grade 1 which is a mild strain with only a few fibres torn, to Grade 2 which is a moderate strain with significant tearing but the muscle is still intact, to Grade 3 which is a complete rupture of the muscle. Symptoms include sudden sharp pain during activity, tenderness when pressing on the muscle, swelling and sometimes bruising. Common locations include the hamstrings at the back of the thigh, the quadriceps at the front of the thigh, the calf muscles and the groin area. Physiotherapy treatment includes RICE in the early stage followed by progressive strengthening and gradual return to sport.

Hamstring Strain:
The hamstrings are the muscles at the back of the thigh. A hamstring strain is one of the most common sports injuries especially in sprinting sports like football, athletics and basketball. It causes sudden sharp pain at the back of the thigh during fast running or kicking. The player usually has to stop immediately. Physiotherapy focuses on progressive loading exercises to heal the muscle and prevent re-injury which is very common with hamstring strains.

Quadriceps Strain:
The quadriceps are the muscles at the front of the thigh. A strain here causes pain at the front of the thigh and difficulty straightening the knee. It commonly occurs during sprinting, kicking or jumping activities.

Calf Strain (Gastrocnemius Strain):
This is a tear in the calf muscles at the back of the lower leg. It causes sudden pain in the calf often described as feeling like being kicked or hit from behind. It is common in sports involving sudden acceleration like tennis, squash and basketball. Physiotherapy includes progressive calf strengthening and stretching before return to sport.

Groin Strain (Adductor Strain):
The adductor muscles run along the inner thigh and bring the legs together. A groin strain causes pain in the inner thigh and groin area especially when bringing the legs together or kicking. It is very common in footballers. Chronic groin pain in athletes requires careful assessment to rule out other causes.

TENDON INJURIES:

Achilles Tendinopathy:
This means the Achilles tendon which connects the calf muscles to the heel bone has become damaged and painful from overuse. It causes pain and stiffness at the back of the heel especially in the morning and at the start of exercise that often eases as the tendon warms up. It is very common in runners. Physiotherapy treatment centres around specific eccentric heel drop exercises which have strong evidence for healing the tendon.

Achilles Tendon Rupture:
This means the Achilles tendon has completely torn. It causes sudden severe pain at the back of the ankle often described as feeling like being kicked or shot. The patient cannot stand on their toes on the affected side. It commonly occurs in middle aged recreational athletes who play sports intermittently. Treatment can be surgical or non-surgical followed by an extended physiotherapy rehabilitation program.

Patellar Tendinopathy (Jumper's Knee):
This means the tendon connecting the kneecap to the shin bone has become painful from repetitive jumping and loading. It causes pain just below the kneecap that is worse at the start of activity, may ease during activity but returns after. It is common in basketball, volleyball and athletics. Physiotherapy uses specific loading exercises to rehabilitate the tendon.

Tennis Elbow (Lateral Epicondylitis):
Despite the name this condition is not limited to tennis players. It means the tendons attaching to the outer part of the elbow have become painful from repetitive gripping, twisting and lifting activities. It causes pain on the outer side of the elbow that can radiate down the forearm. It is common in construction workers, mechanics, cooks and office workers who use a mouse extensively. Physiotherapy includes specific strengthening exercises for the forearm muscles.

Golfer's Elbow (Medial Epicondylitis):
This is similar to tennis elbow but affects the inner side of the elbow. It causes pain on the inner side of the elbow from repetitive gripping and wrist flexion activities. It is common in golfers, climbers and people who do heavy manual work involving gripping.

LIGAMENT INJURIES:

Ankle Sprain:
An ankle sprain means the ligaments supporting the ankle joint have been overstretched or torn. It is one of the most common sports injuries. The most common type is a lateral ankle sprain where the ankle rolls inward injuring the ligaments on the outer side. It causes pain, swelling and bruising around the ankle and difficulty weight bearing. Most ankle sprains respond well to physiotherapy which includes balance training, strengthening and gradual return to sport. Inadequately rehabilitated ankle sprains often lead to chronic ankle instability and repeated sprains.

Knee Ligament Injuries:
See the dedicated knee conditions file for detailed information on ACL, PCL, MCL and LCL injuries. These are common in contact sports and sports involving pivoting and cutting movements.

OVERUSE AND STRESS INJURIES:

Shin Splints (Medial Tibial Stress Syndrome):
Shin splints is a term used to describe pain along the inner border of the shin bone. It is caused by overloading of the bone and surrounding muscles from repetitive impact activities like running and jumping. It is very common in new runners who increase their training too quickly. The pain is felt during and after running and the shin is tender when pressed. Treatment includes relative rest, correction of training errors, strengthening exercises and gradual return to running.

Stress Fracture:
A stress fracture is a tiny crack in a bone caused by repetitive loading rather than a single traumatic event. In athletes the shin bone, foot bones and hip are common sites. The pain builds gradually, is worse during activity and improves with rest. If activity continues the crack can progress to a complete fracture. Complete rest from the aggravating activity is essential followed by a gradual return to training under physiotherapy guidance.

Iliotibial Band Syndrome (ITBS):
This is inflammation of the iliotibial band on the outer side of the knee. It is the most common overuse injury in runners. It causes sharp pain on the outer side of the knee that typically starts after a specific distance of running. Physiotherapy includes hip strengthening exercises, ITB and hip stretching and temporary modification of training load.

Runner's Knee (Patellofemoral Pain Syndrome):
See the knee conditions file for detailed information. It is one of the most common running injuries causing pain around the kneecap.

Plantar Fasciitis:
The plantar fascia is a thick band of tissue running along the bottom of the foot from the heel to the toes. Plantar fasciitis means this tissue has become inflamed and painful. It causes severe heel pain especially with the first steps in the morning or after periods of rest. It is common in runners, people who stand for long hours and those who are overweight. Physiotherapy includes calf and plantar fascia stretching, foot strengthening exercises, footwear advice and sometimes taping.

GYM SPECIFIC INJURIES:

Shoulder Impingement from Overhead Lifting:
Repeatedly lifting weights overhead with poor technique can compress the rotator cuff tendons in the shoulder causing impingement and pain. Physiotherapy focuses on correcting lifting technique, strengthening the rotator cuff and shoulder stabilizing muscles and improving shoulder posture.

Lower Back Injury from Deadlifts or Squats:
Poor technique during heavy compound gym exercises like deadlifts and squats is a common cause of lower back injuries in gym users. Rounding the lower back during these exercises places excessive stress on the spinal discs and muscles. Physiotherapy addresses pain management, core strengthening and technique correction before returning to heavy lifting.

Wrist and Forearm Injuries from Gym Activities:
Exercises like bench press, push ups and heavy curls can strain the wrist and forearm tendons especially when technique is poor or volume is increased too quickly. Physiotherapy includes relative rest, specific strengthening and technique correction.

Muscle Soreness versus Muscle Injury:
Delayed onset muscle soreness or DOMS is the normal aching and stiffness felt in muscles 24 to 48 hours after an intense or unfamiliar workout. It is caused by microscopic damage to muscle fibres during exercise which is a normal part of the muscle strengthening process. DOMS is not an injury and resolves within 3 to 5 days. A muscle injury causes immediate sharp pain during the activity itself, is more localized and is often associated with swelling and bruising.

RETURN TO SPORT:

Return to Sport Criteria:
Before a patient is cleared to return to their sport after injury physiotherapists assess several factors. These include full pain free range of motion, adequate muscle strength usually at least 90 percent of the uninjured side, normal balance and proprioception, the ability to perform sport specific movements without pain and the patient's own psychological readiness to return. Returning to sport too early significantly increases the risk of re-injury.

Sports Specific Rehabilitation:
Towards the end of rehabilitation the physiotherapist designs exercises that mimic the specific demands of the patient's sport. For example a footballer will progress to running, cutting, kicking and ball work before being cleared to return to full training. A swimmer will progress through specific shoulder strengthening and stroke technique correction before returning to full training.
