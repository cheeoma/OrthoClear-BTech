REAL ORTHOPAEDIC PHYSIOTHERAPY CASE EXAMPLES FROM NIGERIAN TEACHING HOSPITAL

CASE 1: POST CORRECTIVE OSTEOTOMY KNEE REHABILITATION
Patient: Adult female
Diagnosis: Post corrective osteotomy of the right knee
Functional Status on Arrival: Patient has been on bed rest since surgery. Katz Index score 3 out of 6 meaning the patient needs help with toileting, dressing and bathing but can feed independently. Transfer ability is zero meaning the patient cannot move from bed to chair independently.

List of Problems:
1. Mild right knee pain
2. Reduced knee range of motion
3. Reduced muscle strength
4. Impaired activities of daily living performance
5. Difficulty with ambulation meaning difficulty walking

Assessment: Inability to ambulate and impaired ADL performance post corrective osteotomy.

Treatment Given:
Free active and assisted active exercises to all joints of the right lower limb. Isometric quadriceps strengthening. Sitting reeducation from long sitting to high sitting on the bed which was well tolerated. Standing reeducation with therapist support. Standing reeducation with Zimmer frame. Walking reeducation with axillary crutches. Patient progressed to being able to walk with axillary crutches and has procured her own crutches.

What This Means For The Patient:
After your knee surgery you were unable to walk or do many things on your own. Your physiotherapy program started with gentle exercises for your knee and leg while lying in bed. Then you gradually learned to sit up, stand with support and finally walk with crutches. This step by step approach is important because it allows your body to heal properly while gradually regaining strength and independence.

CASE 2: LOWER BACK AND BILATERAL KNEE PAIN IN AN ELDERLY PATIENT
Patient: 80 year old female
History: Lower back pain and bilateral knee pain for several years. Has been on physiotherapy since 3 months ago but now comes fortnightly as condition has improved.
Presentation: Patient walked into clinic without any pathological gait meaning she walked in a normal pattern with no obvious limp or abnormal movement. Reports occasional feeling in the lumbo-sacral region. Occasional stiffness following prolonged sitting. Lower back pain rated 4 out of 10 on numeric pain rating scale. Bilateral knee pain rated 0 out of 10 on numeric pain rating scale meaning no knee pain at this visit. Right knee pain rated 5 out of 10.

Assessment: Lumbar spondylosis and bilateral knee osteoarthritis. Patient improving with treatment.

X-ray Findings:
Reduced lumbar lordosis meaning the normal curve of the lower back has straightened. Decreased joint space between L2 and L3. Early osteophytes on L3. Decreased height of L5. Osteophytes in knee joints. Narrowing of knee joint space. Posterior calcification bilaterally.

Treatment:
Infrared radiation to lumbo-sacral region 10 repetitions. Soft tissue manipulation with analgesic cream to the lower back and right knee joint. Interferential therapy and ultrasound to knee joint. Back extension exercises in sitting 5 repetitions. Active range of motion exercises both knee joints in prone position 10 repetitions each. Gentle bilateral hamstring stretching 5 repetitions each. Bicycle ergometry with resistance 3 sets of 6 minutes. Home exercise programme. Patient educated on back care and need for microbreaks after sitting for one hour.

What This Means For The Patient:
You have wear and tear in both your lower back and your knees which is very common at your age. The good news is you are already improving significantly after your physiotherapy sessions. Your knee pain is now zero and your back pain has reduced. Your X-rays show the expected changes of aging in your spine and knees. Your treatment combines heat therapy, electrical treatment, exercises and gentle hands on techniques to keep your joints moving well and reduce pain. The exercises you do at home between sessions are very important for maintaining your progress. Remember to take breaks from sitting every hour.

CASE 3: CERVICAL RADICULITIS WITH RIGHT SHOULDER OSTEOARTHRITIS
Patient: 49 year old being managed for cervical radiculitis and right shoulder osteoarthritis
Presentation: Presents today with right shoulder pain that has eased off. Limited right shoulder range of motion. Right shoulder pain rated 4 out of 10 on VAS. Right shoulder active range of motion 0 to 155 degrees of flexion compared to left shoulder 0 to 175 degrees. Right shoulder active range of motion 0 to 170 degrees on the other measurement.

Treatment:
Infrared radiation 15 repetitions to right shoulder. Longitudinal distraction and posterior gliding of the right shoulder joint. Soft tissue manipulation with topical cream. Wall ladder exercises 10 repetitions. Rhythmic stabilization exercises for shoulder. Range of motion exercises with Theraband. Home exercise programme.

What This Means For The Patient:
You have two things going on. First a nerve in your neck is irritated which is the cervical radiculitis and this can send pain down into your shoulder and arm. Second your right shoulder joint itself shows wear and tear changes called osteoarthritis. Your shoulder movement has improved significantly since you started treatment. Your physiotherapist is using gentle pulling and gliding movements to improve the shoulder joint movement combined with exercises to strengthen the muscles around the shoulder. The wall exercises and Theraband exercises at home are very important for maintaining your shoulder movement between sessions.

CASE 4: LUMBAR SPONDYLODISCITIS WITH RADICULOPATHY
Patient: Adult patient walking into clinic not independently but is observed to have some special gait pattern.
Lumbar region findings: Lumbar scoliosis meaning sideways curve of the lower back. Limited lumbar range of motion. Prone instability test positive meaning the lower back is unstable in certain positions. Digital pressure elicits pain on some spinal levels. Nerve pathways very difficult to assess. Impression suggests lumbar spine spondylodiscitis and lumbar low back pain with 44 percent and 56.99 percent disability rating.

Treatment:
Vertical oscillatory pressure L1 to S1 region using Grade 1 and Grade 2 manipulation techniques. Interferential therapy manipulation to affected levels. Cryotherapy to lumbo-sacral region 15 repetitions. Soft tissue manipulation. Lumbar stabilization exercises in crook lying 10 repetitions 5 seconds each. Side lying with pillows under pelvis 30 minutes. Left lateral stretch on wall bar. Patient education on ergonomics and back care. Home exercise programme.

What This Means For The Patient:
You have inflammation and degeneration in the discs and bones of your lower back which is causing significant pain and disability. Your back is not as stable as it should be and your movement is limited. Your physiotherapist is using gentle pressure techniques on the specific spinal levels affected, electrical treatment, ice therapy and hands on massage to reduce the pain and inflammation. The stabilization exercises you do at home are very important because they strengthen the deep muscles that support and protect your spine from further injury. You have also been taught about ergonomics which means how to position and move your body correctly during daily activities to protect your back.

CASE 5: BILATERAL KNEE OSTEOARTHRITIS TREATMENT
Patient: Adult patient with bilateral knee osteoarthritis
Treatment documented:
Cryotherapy to knee joint. Bilaterally lateral traction to bilateral knee joints 15 minutes with 5 kilogram weight. Sustained traction in high sitting 10 minutes with 4 kilogram weight. Knee joint in high sitting with weight and assisted active exercise. Free active mobilisation exercise. Knee strengthening exercises quadriceps 2 sets 10 repetitions. Soft tissue manipulation. Patient education on dietary control. Home exercise programme. Knee brace recommended.

What This Means For The Patient:
You have arthritis in both your knees which means the cushioning cartilage inside your knee joints has worn away. Your treatment involves applying cold packs to reduce pain and swelling, gentle traction which means a pulling force to create more space inside the knee joint and take pressure off the worn surfaces, exercises to strengthen the muscles around the knee especially the quadriceps at the front of the thigh and hands on massage. A knee brace has been recommended to give your knee extra support during activities. You have also been given advice on diet because maintaining a healthy weight reduces the load on your knee joints significantly. Losing even a small amount of weight can make a big difference to knee pain.

CASE 6: PHYSIOTHERAPY FOR FUNCTIONAL RECOVERY AFTER FRACTURE
Patient: 88 year old woman who had open reduction and internal fixation surgery for right distal femoral fracture 3 months ago. Was discharged home on wheelchair due to heaviness and pain in the unaffected limb. She reported to orthopaedics clinic and was referred back to physiotherapy.
Presentation: Patient was brought into the clinic on a wheelchair though she already has a Zimmer frame. She is doing well with no jaundice or dehydration. No pedal oedema meaning no swelling in the feet. Required assistance to move from the wheelchair to the bed with a Zimmer frame. Reports 4 out of 10 pain in the left knee which is the unaffected limb. Used to have mild pain before the trauma.

Assessment:
Full upper limb assessment normal. Head and neck assessment normal. Thorax normal. Blood pressure 135 over 70 mmHg. Pulse rate 92 beats per minute. Right lower limb which is the affected limb: hip joint active range of motion is assessed as active and full and pain free. Surgical scar on knee joint has healed. No swelling or abnormality. Knee ROM 0 to 180 degrees actively. Yet to take check X-ray but bone healing appears good clinically.

What This Means For The Patient:
After your surgery to fix your broken thigh bone your right leg which was operated on is actually doing very well. The scar has healed nicely, there is no swelling and you can bend your knee fully. The pain you are feeling is actually in your left knee which was not operated on. This is very common after major lower limb surgery because the other leg has been compensating and working harder than usual. Your physiotherapy will continue to help you regain independence in walking and transfer movements and manage the left knee discomfort.

CASE 7: CHILD WITH INABILITY TO WALK REFERRED FROM COMMUNITY
Patient: Oyebode Dorcas, Female, 1 and a half years old
History: Patient was referred from one of the community outreach centres of the hospital on account of inability to walk due to left foot weakness. She was delivered safely through a spontaneous vaginal delivery in an uneventful delivery. She cried immediately and there was no history of jaundice. She achieved neck control, sitting, crawling at the appropriate age but has been unable to walk. Past medical history shows no history of intramuscular injection, complete immunisation. Social history shows both parents engage in farm produce business, first child of the family. Patient was brought into the clinic by both parents already a year old and ambulatory age.

Impression: Delayed ambulation due to left foot weakness and possible lower limb deformity.

Plan of Management:
To address and prevent further contractures. Correction of deformity. To train independent walking as an immediate and long term goal.

Means of Treatment:
Passive mobilisation of the left ankle interphalangeal joints. Passive stretching of the left tendo achilles and posterior muscles. Weight transfer on the left foot in bridging and standing position. Standing education training for correct foot placement. Walking education with support. Fabrication of ankle foot splint.

What This Means For The Patient and Parents:
Your daughter has difficulty walking because her left foot and leg are not as strong as they should be and there may be some tightness in the muscles and joints of the foot. The physiotherapy program will gently stretch the tight muscles and joints of her foot and ankle, train her to put weight on the foot correctly and gradually guide her toward walking independently. A special splint for her ankle and foot will be made to help hold the foot in the correct position while she learns to walk. With consistent treatment and your support at home there is a good chance she will achieve independent walking.
