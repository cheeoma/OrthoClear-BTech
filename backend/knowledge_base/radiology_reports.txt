RADIOLOGY REPORTS AND INVESTIGATIONS IN ORTHOPAEDIC PHYSIOTHERAPY

UNDERSTANDING MRI REPORTS:

What is an MRI?
MRI stands for Magnetic Resonance Imaging. It is a scan that uses strong magnets and radio waves to take very detailed pictures of the inside of your body without using radiation. It is especially useful for looking at soft tissues like muscles, nerves, discs and ligaments that do not show well on regular X-rays. When your physiotherapist or doctor says they want an MRI it means they want a very clear picture of what is happening inside your spine or joints.

What is an X-ray?
An X-ray is a quick scan that takes pictures of your bones. It is good for seeing fractures, joint spaces and bony changes. It uses a small amount of radiation. An AP view means the picture was taken from the front. A Lateral view means it was taken from the side. AP and Lateral together give a complete picture of the joint or bone from two different angles.

CERVICAL SPINE MRI FINDINGS:

Cervical Spondylotic Changes:
This means the bones and discs in your neck are showing signs of wear and tear from aging. The word spondylotic just means age related wear. It is very common in adults above 40 years. It does not always cause symptoms but when it does it can cause neck pain, stiffness and sometimes tingling or pain in the arms.

Disc Desiccation:
This means the cushioning discs between the neck bones have lost some of their water content and become drier and less flexible than normal. Think of it like a sponge drying out. Healthy discs are plump and moist. Desiccated discs are thinner and less shock absorbent. This is a normal part of aging but can contribute to neck pain and stiffness.

Disc Bulge:
A disc bulge means the cushion between two spinal bones has pushed outward slightly beyond its normal boundary. Unlike a full herniation where the inner part of the disc breaks through, a bulge means the outer wall is still intact but is pushing outward. A mild disc bulge may cause no symptoms at all. A larger bulge pressing on nerves can cause pain, tingling or numbness.

Facet Joint Arthrosis:
The facet joints are small joints at the back of each spinal bone that help guide movement of the spine. Arthrosis means these joints have become worn and roughened due to aging. It can cause localized pain and stiffness in the neck or back especially with certain movements.

Ventral Theca Sac Compression:
The theca sac is a protective fluid filled bag that surrounds and protects the spinal cord and nerve roots inside the spinal canal. Ventral means from the front. Compression of the ventral theca sac means a disc bulge or bone spur at the front of the spinal canal is pressing slightly on this protective sac. Mild compression is common and often causes no symptoms. More significant compression can irritate the nerves inside.

Neuro-foraminal Narrowing:
The neuro-foramina are the openings on the sides of the spine through which the nerve roots exit to go to the arms and legs. Narrowing of these openings means the space available for the nerves has reduced. This can happen due to disc bulges, bone spurs or joint enlargement. When a nerve is compressed in a narrowed foramen it can cause pain, tingling or numbness that travels down the arm or leg depending on which level is affected.

Straightening of Cervical Lordosis:
The normal neck has a gentle forward curve called lordosis. When this curve straightens out it is often a sign of muscle spasm protecting the neck from pain. The muscles tighten up and pull the neck straight. This finding on an MRI or X-ray does not mean there is a serious problem. It usually resolves as the underlying cause is treated and the muscles relax.

C4-C6 Vertebral Height Reduction:
This means the bones at the C4, C5 and C6 levels of the neck are slightly shorter in height than normal. This can happen due to long term disc degeneration compressing the space between the bones. It is associated with spondylotic changes and age related wear.

LUMBOSACRAL SPINE MRI FINDINGS:

Lumbar Spondylotic Changes:
This means the lower back bones and discs are showing wear and tear. It is extremely common in adults especially those above 40 years, those who do heavy physical work and those who sit for long hours. The changes include disc desiccation, disc bulges, bone spurs called osteophytes and joint arthrosis.

Marginal Osteophytes:
Osteophytes are small bony growths that form along the edges of bones in response to wear and tear. Think of them as the body trying to stabilize a worn joint by growing extra bone. They are very common in older adults. Small osteophytes usually cause no symptoms. Larger ones can press on nerves and cause pain or stiffness.

Ligamentum Flavum Hypertrophy:
The ligamentum flavum is a thick ligament that runs inside the spinal canal along the back wall. Hypertrophy means it has thickened and enlarged. This can happen with age and repeated stress on the spine. A thickened ligamentum flavum takes up more space inside the spinal canal and can contribute to spinal stenosis by pressing on the nerves from behind.

L4-5 Disc Bulge with Neuro-foraminal Narrowing and Nerve Compression:
This is one of the most common findings in patients with lower back pain and leg pain. It means the disc between the 4th and 5th lumbar bones is bulging outward and pressing on the nerves that pass through the openings at that level. The nerve that is most commonly affected at L4-5 is the L5 nerve root. When this nerve is compressed it causes pain, tingling or numbness that typically runs from the lower back through the buttock and down the outer side of the leg to the top of the foot and big toe. This pattern of pain is called L5 radiculopathy.

L3-4 Disc Bulge with Mild Theca Sac Compression:
This means the disc between the 3rd and 4th lumbar bones is bulging and causing mild pressure on the protective sac around the spinal nerves. Mild compression at this level often causes lower back pain and sometimes pain in the front of the thigh. When the L3 or L4 nerve root is affected the pain can radiate to the inner thigh and knee area.

L5-S1 Disc Bulge:
This means the disc between the last lumbar bone and the first sacral bone is bulging. The S1 nerve root passes through this area. When it is compressed it typically causes pain that runs through the buttock and down the back of the thigh and calf to the outer foot and little toe. This is the classic sciatica pattern.

Impression in MRI Report:
The impression section at the end of an MRI report is the radiologist's summary of the most important findings. It is the conclusion of the report. For example an impression that says spondylotic changes with multilevel degenerative disc disease worse at L4-5 means the overall conclusion is that there is age related wear throughout the spine but it is most significant at the L4-5 level.

UNDERSTANDING X-RAY REPORTS IN PHYSIOTHERAPY:

Reduced Lumbar Lordosis:
The normal lower back has an inward curve called lordosis. When an X-ray shows reduced or straightened lumbar lordosis it means this curve has flattened out. Like in the neck this is often a sign of muscle spasm or pain causing the muscles to guard and pull the spine straight. It usually improves as pain and muscle tension reduce with physiotherapy treatment.

Decreased Joint Space:
Joint space on an X-ray refers to the gap between two bones. This gap represents the cartilage and other soft tissues between the bones. When the joint space is decreased it means the cartilage has thinned or worn away. This is a classic sign of osteoarthritis. In the knee, decreased joint space is seen in patients with knee pain and difficulty walking.

Osteophytes on X-ray:
Osteophytes appearing on X-ray look like small bony spurs or lips growing from the edges of the bones. They are a sign of arthritis and joint degeneration. In the spine they can cause stiffness and sometimes nerve compression.

Normal Joint Articulation:
When an X-ray report says normal joint articulation it means the bones are fitting together properly in the joint with no dislocation or misalignment. This is a reassuring finding.

No Fracture Line Seen:
This means the X-ray shows no evidence of a broken bone. This is a very reassuring finding especially after an injury or fall.

Posterior Calcification:
Calcification means calcium deposits have formed in a soft tissue area. Posterior calcification in the knee area may indicate calcium deposits in the tendons or ligaments behind the knee. This can cause pain and stiffness.

RADIOLOGY POSTING CASE EXAMPLES FROM NIGERIAN TEACHING HOSPITAL:

Case 1: Right Ankle Pain Following a Fall
A 49 year old female patient presented with right ankle pain following a fall with twisting of the right ankle. An X-ray of the right ankle was taken in both the AP and Lateral views. The X-ray showed a normal ankle with no fracture line seen and normal joint articulation. This means the bones of the ankle are not broken and are sitting properly in the joint. The pain is therefore coming from the soft tissues such as the ligaments that were stretched or torn during the fall. This type of injury is called an ankle sprain. Physiotherapy treatment focuses on reducing swelling, restoring ankle movement and strengthening the surrounding muscles.

Case 2: Lumbo-sacral X-ray Findings
A 36 year old patient had a lumbo-sacral X-ray taken in the AP and Lateral views. The X-ray showed reduced lumbar lordosis meaning the normal curve of the lower back has flattened. There was also decreased joint space between L2 and L3 bones and early onset osteophytes forming on L3 meaning small bony growths are beginning to appear. There was also decreased height of the L5 bone. These findings together suggest early degenerative changes in the lower back. Physiotherapy focuses on reducing pain, improving posture and strengthening the core muscles to support the spine.

Case 3: Bilateral Hip and Lumbo-sacral Pain
A 80 year old patient presented with painful hip joint on both sides and lumbo-sacral pain. An X-ray of the lumbo-sacral spine and pelvis was taken. The X-ray showed osteophytes meaning bony spurs, narrowing of the joint space and posterior calcification bilaterally meaning calcium deposits on both sides. These findings are consistent with osteoarthritis of the hip joints and degenerative changes in the lower back. Physiotherapy treatment includes gentle range of motion exercises, strengthening exercises for the hip and core muscles and advice on joint protection during daily activities.

Case 4: Right Elbow Injury
A 11 year old male patient with right elbow injury one year ago being managed for post immobilisation stiffness and possible dislocation. An X-ray of the right elbow joint was taken in both AP and Lateral views. The X-ray showed a healed impacted trans-condylar fracture of the right humerus meaning the bone had broken and has now healed. Callus was noted on the fracture line meaning new bone has formed over the break site as part of the healing process. There was no displacement meaning the bones healed in proper alignment. Physiotherapy focuses on restoring elbow movement and strength after the fracture has healed.

Case 5: Bilateral Knee Pain
A 67 year old female patient presented with bilateral knee pain and difficulty in standing from sitting and walking. An X-ray of both knees was taken in anteroposterior and lateral views. The X-ray showed osteophytes, narrowing of the joint space and posterior calcification bilaterally. These findings are consistent with osteoarthritis of both knees. Physiotherapy treatment includes quadriceps strengthening exercises, knee mobilisation, advice on weight management and activity modification.

CERVICAL SPINE MRI CASE EXAMPLE FROM LAUTECH TEACHING HOSPITAL OYO STATE NIGERIA:

Patient: Adegun Olaitan, 48 years Female
Referring Doctor: Dr Alaofin
Clinical Information: Cervical and lumbosacral spondylotic radiculopathy

Cervical Spine MRI Findings Summary:
The MRI showed straightening of the normal cervical lordosis which may be related to muscle spasm. This means the normal curve of the neck has straightened out likely because the neck muscles are tight and protecting the area. Spondylotic changes were seen throughout the cervical spine in the form of disc osteophyte complex with varying degrees of disc desiccation meaning the discs have dried out and bony spurs have formed. There is disc bulge with facet joint arthrosis causing compression on the protective sac around the spinal cord. There is reduction in height of the C4 to C6 bones. No compression fractures were seen.

At the C2-3 level there is a symmetrical disc bulge but the nerve openings are preserved meaning no nerve compression at this level. At C3-4 there is a disc bulge causing mild compression of the protective sac. At C4-5, C5-6 and C6-7 there are symmetrical disc bulges causing mild compression. The nerve openings at all these levels are preserved.

What This Means For The Patient:
Your neck scan shows normal aging changes in the bones and discs of your neck. The discs which are the cushions between the neck bones have become a bit drier and thinner than ideal and small bony growths have formed around them. Some of these changes are pressing mildly on the protective covering around the spinal cord but the actual openings where the nerves exit the spine are still clear. Your neck muscles have tightened up and straightened your neck curve as a protective response to the pain. Physiotherapy treatment will focus on relieving the muscle tension, improving neck movement and strengthening the neck and upper back muscles to better support your spine.

Lumbosacral Spine MRI Findings Summary:
The MRI showed straightening of the normal lumbar lordosis which may be due to reflex muscle spasm from pain. There are marginal osteophytes meaning bony spurs at the edges of the spinal bones. There is varying degrees of disc desiccation meaning the lower back discs have dried out. There is facet joint arthrosis and thickening of the ligamentum flavum ligament throughout the lower back. At L4-5 there is circumferential disc bulge with compression of the protective nerve sac together with thickening of the ligament and narrowing of the nerve openings with moderate compression of the exiting nerves. This is the most significant level. At L3-4 there is disc bulge with mild compression. At L1-2 and L2-3 and L5-S1 there is disc bulge without significant nerve compromise.

What This Means For The Patient:
Your lower back scan shows wear and tear changes throughout the spine that are most significant at the L4-5 level. At this level the cushion between the bones is bulging and pressing on the nerves that come out of your spine. This is likely causing the pain that travels down your leg. The bony spurs and thickened ligament are also contributing to the narrowing of the space around the nerves. Physiotherapy treatment will focus on reducing nerve irritation, improving posture, strengthening the core muscles and teaching you how to protect your spine during daily activities.
