PHYSIOTHERAPY TREATMENTS AND PROCEDURES

ELECTROTHERAPY TREATMENTS:

TENS (Transcutaneous Electrical Nerve Stimulation):
This is a treatment where small sticky pads called electrodes are placed on the skin over the painful area. A small machine sends mild electrical impulses through the skin to reduce pain. The patient feels a mild tingling sensation. It is safe, painless and commonly used for chronic pain conditions like arthritis, back pain and neck pain. It works by blocking pain signals from reaching the brain and stimulating the body to produce its own natural pain relieving chemicals called endorphins.

Interferential Therapy (IFT):
This is similar to TENS but uses a higher frequency electrical current that penetrates deeper into the tissues. It is used for deeper pain, muscle spasms and swelling. Four electrodes are placed around the painful area and the crossing electrical currents create a comfortable buzzing sensation deep in the tissue. It is commonly used for joint pain, back pain and post-surgical swelling.

Ultrasound Therapy (US):
This treatment uses high frequency sound waves that penetrate deep into soft tissues like muscles, tendons and ligaments. The patient feels gentle warmth in the area being treated. It helps reduce inflammation, break down scar tissue and speed up healing of soft tissue injuries. It is commonly used for tendinitis, muscle strains, ligament sprains and joint conditions. The physiotherapist moves a small probe over the skin with gel applied to help the sound waves enter the tissue.

Short Wave Diathermy (SWD):
This treatment uses high frequency radio waves to generate deep heat inside muscles and joints. It penetrates much deeper than a hot pack and is used for chronic joint conditions like osteoarthritis and deep muscle pain. It helps improve blood circulation, reduce muscle spasm and decrease joint stiffness. It cannot be used on patients with metal implants, pacemakers or during pregnancy.

Microwave Diathermy (MWD):
Similar to short wave diathermy but uses microwave energy to heat deep tissues. It is used for conditions like muscle spasm, joint stiffness and chronic pain. Like SWD it cannot be used near metal implants or on pregnant patients.

Infrared Radiation (IR):
This treatment uses a heat lamp that emits infrared rays to warm the surface of the skin and the superficial muscles underneath. It helps relax tight muscles, reduce muscle spasm, improve local blood circulation and reduce mild pain. It is commonly used before exercise or manual therapy to warm up the tissues and make them more flexible.

Neuromuscular Electrical Stimulation (NMES):
This treatment uses electrical current to make muscles contract when they are too weak to contract on their own. It is commonly used after surgery, stroke or prolonged immobilization when muscles have become very weak or wasted. It helps prevent muscle wasting, re-educate weakened muscles and improve muscle strength.

MANUAL THERAPY TREATMENTS:

Joint Mobilization:
This is a hands-on technique where the physiotherapist uses their hands to gently move a stiff joint through its range of motion. The movement is slow and controlled and within the patient's comfort. It helps restore normal joint movement, reduce stiffness and decrease pain. It is commonly used for stiff spinal joints, knee joints and shoulder joints.

Joint Manipulation:
This is similar to joint mobilization but uses a faster, more forceful movement applied to a stiff joint. It often produces a popping or cracking sound which is just gas being released from the joint fluid and is harmless. It is used to restore joint movement and reduce pain quickly. It requires specialized training and is not suitable for all patients.

Soft Tissue Mobilization (STM):
This is a hands-on massage technique used to release tightness in muscles, fascia and other soft tissues. The physiotherapist uses their hands to apply pressure and movement to tight or knotted areas of muscle. It helps reduce muscle spasm, improve flexibility and increase blood flow to the area.

Myofascial Release (MFR):
Fascia is a thin layer of connective tissue that wraps around all muscles and organs in the body. When fascia becomes tight or restricted it can cause pain and restricted movement. Myofascial release is a gentle sustained pressure technique applied to these restricted areas to release the tightness and restore normal movement. The pressure is held for an extended period until the physiotherapist feels the tissue relax and release.

Proprioceptive Neuromuscular Facilitation (PNF) Stretching:
This is an advanced stretching technique that combines muscle contraction and relaxation to achieve a greater range of motion than regular stretching. The patient contracts the muscle being stretched against resistance provided by the physiotherapist, then relaxes and the physiotherapist stretches the muscle further. It is very effective for improving flexibility and is commonly used in sports rehabilitation.

Traction:
This technique involves gently pulling the spine or a limb to create space between the joints and reduce pressure on compressed nerves and discs. Spinal traction is commonly used for disc herniations and nerve compression in the neck and lower back. It can be applied manually by the physiotherapist or using a special mechanical traction machine.

EXERCISE THERAPY:

Home Exercise Program (HEP):
This is a set of specific exercises the physiotherapist designs for the patient to do at home between clinic sessions. It is a very important part of physiotherapy treatment. The exercises are tailored to the patient's specific condition and goals. Consistency with the home exercise program significantly speeds up recovery. The physiotherapist will demonstrate each exercise and make sure the patient understands how to do them correctly before going home.

Core Stabilization Exercises:
The core refers to the deep muscles of the abdomen, back and pelvis that work together to support and stabilize the spine. Core stabilization exercises strengthen these deep muscles to protect the spine during movement and daily activities. They are commonly prescribed for patients with back pain, disc problems and spinal instability. Examples include pelvic tilts, dead bugs, bird dogs and planks done at the appropriate level for the patient.

Strengthening Exercises:
These are exercises designed to increase the strength of specific muscles that have become weak due to injury, surgery or disuse. They usually start with gentle low resistance exercises and gradually increase in difficulty as the muscle gets stronger. Examples include straight leg raises for the quadriceps, clamshells for the hip muscles and shoulder external rotation exercises for the rotator cuff.

Stretching Exercises:
These exercises are used to lengthen tight muscles and improve flexibility around joints. Tight muscles can restrict joint movement and cause pain. Stretching helps restore normal muscle length and joint range of motion. Each stretch is usually held for 20 to 30 seconds and repeated several times.

Balance and Proprioception Training:
Proprioception means the body's ability to sense its own position in space. After a joint injury especially ankle, knee or shoulder injuries this sense is often impaired. Balance training exercises retrain the nervous system to improve joint position sense and prevent re-injury. Examples include standing on one leg, using a wobble board and performing exercises on unstable surfaces.

Gait Training:
This means training the patient to walk correctly again. It is used after lower limb surgery, fractures, stroke or any condition that has affected the patient's ability to walk normally. The physiotherapist assesses the patient's walking pattern and uses specific exercises and techniques to correct abnormalities and improve walking efficiency.

HEAT AND COLD TREATMENTS:

Moist Heat Pack (MHP):
A warm moist pack applied to the painful area for about 15 to 20 minutes. The heat increases blood flow, relaxes tight muscles and reduces stiffness. It is commonly used before exercises to warm up the tissues. It is not used on acute fresh injuries with swelling as heat can increase swelling.

Cold Pack (CP) or Ice Therapy (Cryotherapy):
An ice pack or cold pack applied to an injured area for about 10 to 15 minutes. Cold reduces blood flow to the area, decreases swelling and numbs the area to reduce pain. It is most useful in the first 24 to 72 hours after an acute injury. The skin should always be protected with a cloth between the ice and skin to prevent ice burns.

RICE Protocol:
RICE stands for Rest, Ice, Compression and Elevation. It is the standard first aid treatment recommended immediately after a soft tissue injury like a sprain or strain.
Rest means stop the activity that caused the injury and avoid putting weight on it.
Ice means apply an ice pack wrapped in a cloth to the injured area for 10 to 15 minutes every 2 hours.
Compression means wrap the injured area with a bandage to reduce swelling.
Elevation means raise the injured limb above the level of the heart to help reduce swelling.

ASSISTIVE DEVICES:

Crutches:
Crutches are walking aids used when a patient cannot put full weight on one or both legs due to injury or surgery. The physiotherapist teaches the patient how to use crutches correctly and safely on flat ground and on stairs. Using crutches incorrectly can cause shoulder and wrist pain so proper instruction is very important.

Walking Frame (Zimmer Frame):
A walking frame provides support and balance for patients who are unsteady on their feet. It is commonly used by elderly patients recovering from hip fractures or surgery and patients with neurological conditions affecting balance. The physiotherapist teaches the correct technique for using the frame safely.

Orthosis and Splints:
An orthosis is a device worn on the body to support, align, correct or protect a body part. Examples include knee braces, ankle supports, wrist splints and lumbar corsets. They are used to protect injured structures, reduce pain and improve function during recovery.

Taping and Strapping:
Physiotherapists use special adhesive tape to support injured joints and muscles, improve joint alignment and reduce pain during activity. Rigid strapping tape is used to restrict movement of unstable joints. Kinesiology tape is a flexible tape used to support muscles and joints without restricting movement. It can also help reduce swelling and improve proprioception.
