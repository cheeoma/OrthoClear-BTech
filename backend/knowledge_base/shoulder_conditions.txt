SHOULDER CONDITIONS IN ORTHOPAEDIC PHYSIOTHERAPY

ROTATOR CUFF CONDITIONS:

Rotator Cuff Tear:
The rotator cuff is a group of four muscles and their tendons that surround the shoulder joint and keep the arm bone firmly in the shoulder socket. A tear means one or more of these tendons have been partially or completely torn. It can happen suddenly from a fall or lifting something heavy, or gradually from repeated overhead activities over many years. Symptoms include shoulder pain especially when lifting the arm, weakness in the shoulder and sometimes a crackling sensation with movement. Physiotherapy after a rotator cuff tear focuses on reducing pain, restoring shoulder movement and strengthening the surrounding muscles. Severe complete tears may require surgery before physiotherapy.

Rotator Cuff Tendinitis:
This means one or more of the rotator cuff tendons have become inflamed and irritated. It causes shoulder pain especially when lifting the arm to the side or overhead. It is common in people who do repeated overhead activities like painters, swimmers, volleyball players and traders who carry loads on their heads. Physiotherapy includes exercises to strengthen the rotator cuff muscles, postural correction and advice on modifying activities.

Rotator Cuff Impingement Syndrome:
This means the rotator cuff tendons are being pinched or squeezed between the bones of the shoulder when the arm is lifted. It causes a painful arc of movement, meaning there is a specific range where lifting the arm is very painful. It is very common in people above 40 years. Physiotherapy focuses on improving shoulder posture, strengthening specific shoulder muscles and correcting movement patterns.

FROZEN SHOULDER:

Frozen Shoulder (Adhesive Capsulitis):
Frozen shoulder means the capsule of tissue surrounding the shoulder joint has become inflamed, thickened and tight. This causes the shoulder to become progressively more stiff and painful over time. It develops in three stages. The first stage called the freezing stage is very painful and movement begins to reduce. The second stage called the frozen stage is less painful but the shoulder is very stiff and difficult to move. The third stage called the thawing stage is when the shoulder slowly starts to loosen up and movement returns. The whole process can take one to three years. It is more common in women, people with diabetes and people between 40 and 60 years. Physiotherapy is very important at each stage to maintain as much movement as possible and manage pain.

SHOULDER INSTABILITY:

Shoulder Dislocation:
This means the head of the arm bone has completely come out of the shoulder socket. It is very painful and the shoulder looks obviously out of shape. It is commonly caused by a fall on an outstretched arm or a direct blow to the shoulder during contact sports. After the shoulder is put back in place by a doctor physiotherapy focuses on restoring movement, strengthening the rotator cuff muscles and preventing future dislocations.

Shoulder Subluxation:
This means the head of the arm bone partially slips out of the shoulder socket but does not completely dislocate. It causes a feeling of the shoulder slipping or giving way. It is common after a stroke where the shoulder muscles become weak, or after repeated shoulder injuries in athletes. Physiotherapy focuses on strengthening the muscles that hold the shoulder in place.

Shoulder Instability:
This means the shoulder joint is loose and does not feel stable during movement. The patient often feels the shoulder slipping, clicking or giving way. It can be caused by repeated dislocations, ligament laxity or muscle weakness. Physiotherapy focuses on strengthening the rotator cuff and surrounding shoulder muscles to provide better dynamic stability.

ACROMIOCLAVICULAR CONDITIONS:

Acromioclavicular Joint Sprain (AC Joint Sprain):
The acromioclavicular joint is where the collarbone meets the top of the shoulder blade. A sprain here is commonly caused by a fall directly onto the shoulder or onto an outstretched hand. It causes pain and tenderness at the top of the shoulder. In mild cases physiotherapy with rest and gradual exercises is sufficient. In severe cases surgery may be needed.

BICEP CONDITIONS:

Biceps Tendinitis:
This means the tendon of the biceps muscle at the front of the upper arm has become inflamed where it attaches to the shoulder. It causes pain at the front of the shoulder that gets worse when lifting or carrying objects with the palm facing upward. It is common in people who do repeated lifting, throwing or overhead activities. Physiotherapy includes rest from aggravating activities, ice therapy and progressive strengthening exercises.

Biceps Tendon Rupture:
This means the biceps tendon has completely torn, usually at the shoulder end. It causes sudden sharp pain, a popping sensation and sometimes visible deformity where the muscle bunches up in the middle of the upper arm. Surgical repair is often needed followed by physiotherapy rehabilitation.

GENERAL SHOULDER CONDITIONS:

Shoulder Bursitis:
There are small fluid filled sacs called bursae in the shoulder that reduce friction between moving parts. When one of these becomes inflamed it is called bursitis. It causes localized shoulder pain and tenderness especially when lifting the arm. It often occurs alongside rotator cuff problems. Physiotherapy includes activity modification, ice therapy and gentle shoulder exercises.

SLAP Lesion (Superior Labrum Anterior to Posterior Tear):
The labrum is a ring of cartilage that deepens the shoulder socket. A SLAP tear means the top part of this cartilage ring has been torn. It causes deep shoulder pain, a clicking or catching sensation and sometimes shoulder instability. It is common in throwing athletes like cricketers and baseball players and also in people who fall on an outstretched arm. Physiotherapy after surgery focuses on gradually restoring shoulder strength and function.
